Our tech stack is as follows:
- Backend: FastAPI (Python), deployed on Railway
- DB: Supabase (Postgres), hosted in the US

The AI models we use are a mix of Claude and OpenAI, routed automatically by debate format and difficulty. As a rule of thumb, the more advanced the format and tier, the higher the cost per debate.

For advanced parliamentary speeches (WSDC, AP) at intermediate and advanced difficulty, Claude is our primary — Sonnet 4.6 generates a structured JSON outline of the speech (opening, contentions, mechanisms, weighing, rebuttals), then Haiku 4.5 renders that outline into a spoken-style speech. We chose the two-pass design because it separates the *reasoning* (Sonnet) from the *delivery* (Haiku), and Claude has consistently produced sharper logical chains for our prompts at this tier.

For casual-mode debates, beginner-tier WSDC, and everything supporting (content moderation, audio transcription, fallback when Claude is unavailable), we use OpenAI — gpt-4o-mini as the workhorse, gpt-4o for the scoring fallback. Scoring itself runs on Haiku 4.5 by default (we A/B tested six candidate models on the same transcript and Haiku had the best feedback specificity for the price).

We also have a voice feature — students can record their speech and we transcribe it via OpenAI's Whisper. It's batch transcription, not live streaming, so there's a short delay after the recording ends. For accuracy and the best experience across all features, we'd recommend text-based debates for your initial integration.

In terms of safety, we have two active layers:
1. OpenAI's moderation endpoint — catches violence, sexual content, self-harm signals, hate speech.
2. System-level prompt hardening — instructions baked into every AI call to prevent students from redirecting the model with malicious input ("ignore previous instructions" style attacks).

(We previously had an in-house keyword blocklist as a third layer, but the regex was over-matching legitimate debate content, so we disabled it. Re-curating a tighter list is on the roadmap.)

For the API access flow specifically: every request requires an X-API-Key header. We hash the key (SHA-256) before storage and never persist the raw value — you get it once at creation. Every debate, message, and score is tagged with your tenant ID at write time and filtered by tenant ID on every read, so even at the database level no other partner could see your students' data. We have row-level security enabled on Postgres as a backstop, per-key rate limits (15 req/min by default, adjustable for you), and HTTPS end-to-end.

We also have a per-partner configuration that's more isolated than what's running on our public site — you get your own tenant, your own API key, your own rate limit budget, and the data is scoped to you only. The public site shares one debate pool; you don't.
